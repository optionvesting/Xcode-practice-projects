DEFINITION: a single option position where the investor doesn't own the underlying stock, nor does the investor own another protecting option on the same underlying stock.

Naked option positions provide more reward than covered positions, but are also more risky.

An example of a naked option is simply buying or selling a call or put option. The value of a naked option can fluxuate rapidly.

An example of a covered option is selling covered calls. First buy 100 shares of a stock, and then sell 1 contract of an out-of-the-money call on the same stock. Since you already own the stock, your position has very limited risk.

Another way to cover an option position is by adding a second "leg" to the position. For example, sell an out-of-the-money put, and then buy another put further out-of-the-money. You still bring in some profit since the put you sold cost more than the put you bought, but your risk is limited.
