Money on hold.

Whenever you place an option trade, you are entering a contract.
➫ A call contract involves buying 100 shares of the underlying stock.
➫ A put contract involves selling 100 shares of the underlying stock.

Depending on what type of position you want to open (long or short, naked or covered, etc.) your broker may require a certain amount of money in your account, that may be placed on hold until you close the position, in order to ensure you can cover the cost of that contract.

This amount of money can vary greatly depending on the type of trade.

Even though most option strategies don't involve actually exercising the option to buy or sell any shares of the underlying stock, you are on the hook to fulfill the contract, so your broker needs to make sure that you have enough cash on hand to do so. The money placed on hold is often referred to as a margin requirement.

The following paragraphs describe how much money (if any) your broker will place on hold for each of the main strategies discussed in these tutorials.

 

Covered calls: extra cash is NOT required.

When you already own 100 shares of the underlying stock for each contract of the option you want to trade, then your position is referred to as “covered.” This principle applies if you want to sell a call on the stock, or buy a protective put for the stock.

With these covered positions, your broker doesn’t require any money on hold because the underlying stock is already in your account. In the event that your option contract gets executed, you already have the shares of stock so you won’t have to buy them.

Later tutorials will describe the specific benefits of and steps to open covered positions. Just remember that they are low risk and thus do not require extra money on hold in your account.

 

Buy a naked call: extra cash is NOT required.

When you buy a call option, you are buying a contract to be able to buy 100 shares of stock at a given strike price. If you choose to actually use that call option to buy 100 shares of the underlying stock, that is called executing the option.

Since you bought the contract, you can choose to execute it anytime you want to before it expires.

It would never make sense to execute your option when it is out-of-the-money. If the option is in-the-money, it may be beneficial to execute it.

In order to execute the call you bought, you need to have enough cash in your account to buy 100 shares of the stock at the strike price. Else, you won't be able to execute the option.

Due to the fact that you bought the option and you decide if/when it will be executed, your broker will not require any extra cash to remain in your account as collateral.

Possible scenarios AT EXPIRATION of your naked long call:

1) the option is in-the-money AND you have enough cash in your account to buy 100 shares of the stock:
➫➫ Your broker will automatically execute the option, buying you 100 shares of stock at the strike price.

2) the option is in-the-money AND you DO NOT have enough cash in your account to buy the 100 shares of stock:
➫➫ DON’T LET THIS HAPPEN!
➫➫ Your option is now worthless since you can’t sell it back to the market maker, nor can your broker execute the option and buy the stock for you.
➫➫ Anytime you own an option that is in the money and getting close to expiration, you should sell it back to the market maker to get some money back if you don’t have enough money in your account to buy 100 shares of the stock at the strike price.

3) the option is out-of-the-money:
➫➫ It expired worthless, and will be removed from your account.

These changes will always be reflected in your account on the Monday after the option expired, with no action required on your part.

 

Buy a naked put: extra cash is NOT required.

When you buy a put option, you are buying a contract to be able to sell 100 shares of stock at a given strike price. If you choose to actually use that put option to sell 100 shares of the underlying stock, that is called executing the option.

Since you bought the contract, you can choose to execute it anytime you want to before it expires.

It would never make sense to execute your option when it is out-of-the-money, but anytime the option is in-the-money, you can choose to execute it.

If you choose to execute your put prior to expiration, then your broker will sell 100 shares of underlying stock for the given strike price.
➫ If you already own 100 shares of the underlying stock, then they will be sold at the strike price of the put option.
➫ If you don't own 100 shares of the underlying stock, executing the put will open a short position on the stock. You will then be required to buy the 100 shares back at some point to close the position. The money you receive in your account from the selling 100 shares of the stock simply stays in your account on hold so you can use it later to buy the shares back.

Due to the fact that you bought the option and you decide if/when it will be executed, your broker will not require any extra cash to remain in your account as collateral.

Possible scenarios AT EXPIRATION of your naked put:

1) the option is in-the-money AND you own 100 shares of the underlying stock:
➫➫ Your broker will automatically execute the option, sell your 100 shares of stock at the strike price, and the money from the sale goes in your account.

2) the option is in-the-money AND you DO NOT own 100 shares of the underlying stock:
➫➫ Your broker will open a short position by selling short 100 shares of the underlying stock. The money received from the sale will go in your account, but remain on hold, since you are required to buy the stock back at some point.
➫➫ If you don't want to sell the stock short, then DO NOT let the put expire in-the-money. Sell it back to the market maker for some premium before it expires.

3) the option is out-of-the-money:
➫➫ It expired worthless, and will be removed from your account.

These changes will always be reflected in your account on the Monday after the option expired, with no action required on your part.

 

Sell a naked call: extra cash IS required.

When you sell a call option, you are selling a contract to the market maker, to be able to buy 100 shares of stock from you at a given strike price.

Now it is the market maker’s choice to execute the option, and he may execute it anytime it is in-the-money (although it is not common for that to happen, it could happen).

If the market maker chooses to actually use that call option to buy 100 shares of the underlying stock FROM YOU, that is called executing the option.

It would never make sense for the market maker to execute the option when it is out-of-the-money.

Since this option could be executed anytime, your broker will require extra cash in your account. The amount of extra cash required to sell a naked call may differ depending on who your broker is.
➫ The typical amount required to sell a naked call is roughly 10% of the value of 100 shares of the underlying stock, plus the amount of premium you receive in your account when you sell the call. Once you have selected your broker and are ready to sell a naked call, be sure to check the margin requirements for the trade.

If you don’t have sufficient cash in your account when you attempt to place the trade, your broker should reject the order.

Possible scenarios AT EXPIRATION of the naked call you sold to the market maker:

1) the option is in-the-money:
➫➫ You are required to purchase 100 shares of the stock at the current market value, and the sell them back to the market maker at the strike price of the option.
➫➫ This will result in a loss for you, so AVOID THIS SITUATION!
➫➫ If you sell a naked call and it is in-the-money approaching expiration, you are best off closing the position by buying it back prior to expiration.

2) the option is out-of-the-money:
➫➫ It expired worthless, and will be removed from your account.
➫➫ This is the desired end state at expiration anytime you sell a naked option.

These changes will always be reflected in your account on Monday after the option expired, with no action required on your part.

 

Sell a naked put: extra cash IS required.

When you sell a put option, you are selling a contract to the market maker, that allows him to sell YOU 100 shares of stock at the given strike price. Another way to think of this agreement is that you will buy the stock from the market maker at the strike price.

Notice that selling a put is essentially the same agreement as buying a naked call.

Since the market maker bought the option, it is his choice to execute the option, and he may execute it anytime it is in-the-money before it expires (although this is not common, it could happen). If the market maker chooses to actually use that put option to sell YOU 100 shares of the underlying stock, that is called executing the option.

It would never make sense for the market maker to execute the option when it is out-of-the-money.

Since this option could be executed anytime, your broker will require extra cash in your account. The amount of extra cash required to sell a naked put may differ depending on who your broker is.
➫ The typical amount required is roughly 10% of the value of 100 shares of the underlying stock at the given strike price, plus the amount of premium you receive in your account when you sell the put. Once you have selected your broker and are ready to sell a naked put, be sure to check the margin requirements for the trade.

If you don’t have sufficient cash in your account when you attempt to place the trade, your broker should reject the order.

Possible scenarios AT EXPIRATION of the naked put you sold to the market maker:

1) the option is in-the-money:
➫➫ You are required to purchase 100 shares of the stock at the given strike price. That is called "getting put the stock."
➫➫ If you don't want to keep the stock and plan to sell it shortly, this will result in a loss for you, so AVOID THIS SITUATION!
➫➫ If you don't mind buying 100 shares of the stock at the strike price, then this situation is an ideal end state for you.

2) the option is out-of-the-money:
➫➫ It expired worthless, and will be removed from your account.
➫➫ This is the usually the desired end state at expiration anytime you sell a naked option.

These changes will always be reflected in your account on Monday after the option expired, with no action required on your part.

 

Credit spreads: extra cash IS required.

Remember, a spread is when you buy one option and sell another on the same underlying stock at the same time. This is a way to limit risk, since it is a way to cover your position.

Each option that you buy and sell is called a different "leg" of the spread. The option you sell is called the short leg (because it is a short position) and the option you buy is called the long leg (because it is a long position).

Typically, if you lose money on one leg of the spread, you will gain money on the other leg, thus lowering risk.

As a result, your broker doesn’t require as much money on hold for a spread as for selling a naked option. The amount of extra cash required (the margin requirement) is simply the difference between the two strike prices multiplied by the number of shares of stock controlled by the spread (100 for 1 contract, 200 for 2 contracts, etc.).

If the short leg of your spread executes, your broker will automatically execute the long leg also, so the entire position closes together, limiting your loss.

Possible scenarios AT EXPIRATION of the credit spread:

1) both options of the spread are in-the-money:
➫➫ Both options will execute automatically, resulting in your max loss, so AVOID THIS SITUATION!
➫➫ If you see your spread is going to be in-the-money approaching expiration, you are best off "rolling" the position to a later expiration date. In order to "roll" your spread, you simply close the spread that is about to expire and open another spread, with the exact same strike prices, that will expire at a later date.
➫➫ When you roll your spread, you take a small loss, but buy more time for the options to expire out-of-the-money, avoiding your maximum loss.

2) the short leg of the spread is in-the-money, and the long leg is out-of-the-money:
➫➫ Both options will execute automatically, resulting in a loss for you, so AVOID THIS SITUATION!
➫➫ If you see any leg of your spread is going to be in-the-money approaching expiration, you are best off closing the position by buying back the option you sold, and selling the option you originally bought, prior to expiration, or rolling the entire spread as described above.

2) both options of the spread are out-of-the-money:
➫➫ They expired worthless, and will be removed from your account.
➫➫ This is the usually the desired end state at expiration anytime you trade a credit spread.

These changes will always be reflected in your account on Monday after the option expired, with no action required on your part.

 

Closing a position before it's too late.

Anytime you don’t want an in-the-money option to execute (as is the case with most option strategy), you should close the trade prior to expiration if the option is in-the-money.

Remember, the last day to trade an option is the last trading day prior to the expiration date. For monthly options that means the 3rd Friday of the month. However, if the market is closed that Friday for a holiday, then the last trading might be Thursday, Wednesday, etc.

Since options CANNOT be traded during pre-market our after-hours trading, the orders must be placed and filled prior to 4:00 pm EST that day.
