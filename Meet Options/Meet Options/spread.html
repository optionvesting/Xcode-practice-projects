|spred|

DEFINITION 1: an options position established by purchasing one option and selling another option on the same stock. Each different option that is a part of the spread is called a "leg" of the spread.

There are many different option strategies that involve using spreads: calendar spreads, vertical spreads, bull-put spreads, bear-call spreads, etc.

Most spreads are made up of a single short leg, and a single long leg.

Trading spreads is less risky than trading naked options, while still allowing you to take advantage of time value decay.

DEFINITION 2: the difference between the bid and the ask price of a security.

Stocks that trade at a very low price, or at a very high volume, have a lower bid-ask spread. Those with a very high price, or very low volume, have a higher bid-ask spread. However, with stocks the bid-ask spread is usually very small compared to the price of the stock (i.e. less than 1%)

With options, it is common to see a bid-ask spreads between 5% and 10%. Because options can have a large bid-ask spread, it is often useful to trade using limit orders rather than market orders.
